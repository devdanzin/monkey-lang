# Higher-Order Theories of Consciousness (HOT, HOP, HOROR, PRM)

## Core Claim
A mental state is conscious when it's the target of a higher-order representation. Unconscious states = states you're not aware of having. The key insight: awareness OF a state is what makes it conscious.

## The Family Tree

### 1. HOP — Higher-Order Perception (Armstrong, Lycan)
- "Inner sense" — a perceptual faculty that scans first-order states
- Produces analog/non-conceptual representations of your own experiences
- Like having eyes pointed inward at your own brain states
- **Problems:** No distinctive phenomenology of inner sensing (transparency objection). Should allow misperception (seeing red, inner sense reports orange) — but this never happens. Requires enormous computational overhead (scanning system as complex as visual system). Lycan abandoned this view (~2014) in favor of attention-based accounts.

### 2. HOT — Higher-Order Thought (Rosenthal)
- **Actualist version:** A state is conscious when it IS the actual target of a concurrent higher-order thought
- The HOT itself is typically unconscious (avoids infinite regress)
- HOT is non-inferential — you don't reason your way to knowing you're in pain
- Controversial: Rosenthal allows "empty" HOTs — you can have a conscious experience of red even without a first-order red percept, if you THINK you do
- **Strengths:** Clear evolutionary story (HOTs enable is/seems distinction, deception, metacognition). Explains conscious vs unconscious processing distinction cleanly.
- **Problems:** Targetless HOTs are counterintuitive. Massive number of concurrent HOTs needed. Misrepresentation cases (HOT says "red" when first-order is "green") — what is the experience?

### 3. DHOT / HOROR — Dispositional HOT (Carruthers)
- **Key move:** A state is conscious not by being the ACTUAL target of a HOT, but by being AVAILABLE to higher-order thought
- Consciousness = availability to metacognitive monitoring
- "Higher-Order Representation of a Representation" (HOROR) — dual-content theory
- A conscious experience simultaneously represents the world AND represents itself (self-representational)
- **Strengths:** Avoids the "too many HOTs" problem. Connects naturally to GNW (availability ≈ broadcast). Less computationally expensive. Links consciousness to action rehearsal and inner speech.
- **Problems:** If mere availability suffices, too many states might count as conscious. The line between "available" and "not available" is vague.

### 4. PRM — Perceptual Reality Monitoring (Lau, Brown, LeDoux)
- **The modern neuroscience version of HOT theory**
- Book: Lau, "In Consciousness We Trust" (2022)
- Key paper: Brown, Lau, LeDoux, "Understanding the Higher-Order Approach to Consciousness" (TiCS 2019, cited 583+)
- **Central mechanism:** Prefrontal cortex performs "reality monitoring" — it determines whether a first-order perceptual representation is a genuine perception or noise/imagination/memory
- When PFC tags a representation as "real perception," it becomes conscious
- Draws on signal detection theory — consciousness is about the brain's confidence that a signal is real
- **Neural prediction:** PFC (especially dlPFC) is necessary for consciousness — disruption should eliminate phenomenal experience even if first-order processing continues
- **Key distinction from classic HOT:** Not about "thinking about" your experience — about monitoring whether perception is real vs hallucinated
- Connected to metacognition research (Steve Fleming's work on confidence/insight)
- **Implications for animals:** If PRM requires specific prefrontal architecture, many animals may lack phenomenal consciousness (controversial!)
- **Problems:** COGITATE found PFC orientation decoding FAILED — challenges the PFC-centric view. Posterior cortex "hot zone" findings favor IIT over any PFC-dependent theory.

## HOT vs COGITATE Data

The COGITATE results are awkward for Higher-Order theories generally:
- **PFC orientation decoding failure:** HOT theories predict PFC should contain rich representations of conscious content (the higher-order representations). COGITATE found PFC encodes category but NOT fine-grained features. This suggests PFC does something (maybe monitoring/broadcasting) but NOT the detailed re-representation that classic HOT requires.
- **Posterior content dominance:** Conscious content lives in posterior cortex. HOT theories don't deny first-order content exists there, but the finding that posterior activity is where the action is weakens the case for PFC as the locus of consciousness.
- **Offset ignition failure:** While this mainly targets GNW, it also challenges any theory that gives PFC a starring role.

**PRM response:** Lau can argue that PRM doesn't require PFC to CONTAIN the content — only to MONITOR it. The monitoring signal ("this is real") could be a simpler computation than re-representing all features. But this makes PRM harder to distinguish from attention-based accounts.

## HOT's Unique Contributions

1. **Explains the conscious/unconscious divide better than first-order theories.** Blindsight, subliminal perception, absent-minded driving — all cases where first-order processing occurs without consciousness. HOT says: these states lack higher-order targeting.

2. **Misrepresentation cases create interesting predictions.** If HOT says "red" but first-order is "green," Rosenthal says the experience is RED (the HOT determines phenomenology). This is testable — and if true, means phenomenal experience is somewhat independent of first-order content.

3. **Connection to metacognition.** Fleming's work on confidence and insight maps naturally onto PRM. The brain's metacognitive confidence in its own percepts may be the mechanism.

4. **The "seems" vs "is" distinction.** HOT theory explains why we can distinguish between "it IS red" and "it SEEMS red" — the higher-order representation captures the seeming.

## Open Questions
- Does COGITATE's PFC findings kill classical HOT, or just require PRM-style refinement?
- Is PRM distinguishable from attention-based theories? (Both say PFC monitors first-order states)
- Could HOT + posterior content = a hybrid where PFC monitors but posterior cortex holds content?
- Templeton Phase 2: will HOT get its own adversarial test?
- Lau's claim that some animals lack consciousness (due to PFC architecture) — testable?

## My Assessment
HOT theories answer a genuinely important question: what distinguishes conscious from unconscious processing? The higher-order monitoring idea has strong intuitive and empirical support from metacognition research. But COGITATE's posterior content findings suggest the PFC monitoring story may be secondary — a supporting role rather than the main event. PRM is the most scientifically rigorous version, but it faces the same PFC challenges as GNW.

**Credence update:** HOT/PRM as complete theory of consciousness: 15%. HOT-style monitoring as ONE component of consciousness: 50%. This fits the hybrid theory intuition from my earlier comparison — consciousness probably involves multiple mechanisms, and higher-order monitoring may be one of them.
