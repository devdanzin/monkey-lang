# Attention Schema Theory (AST) — Michael Graziano

## Core Claim
Consciousness is the brain's simplified internal model of its own attention. Just as the body schema models the body for motor control, the **attention schema** models attention for attentional control. Subjective experience is what this model "looks like from the inside."

## The Three-Part Framework
1. **Brain is an information processor** — processes signals, builds internal models
2. **Attention** = selective enhancement of some signals over others (mechanistic, physical)
3. **Attention schema** = brain's internal model of its own attention — simplified, schematic, lacking mechanistic detail

## Why It Produces "Subjective Experience"
The attention schema describes attention but omits physical implementation details (neurons, synapses, electrochemistry). When higher cognition accesses this model, it finds something that:
- Has no physical properties (because the model doesn't include them)
- Seems located "inside" the self
- Seems to be a non-physical "mental possession" of objects
- Appears to transcend physicality

**This is why consciousness seems mysterious** — not because it IS mysterious, but because the brain's model of its own attention is deliberately simplified, so the system can't see its own machinery.

## The Body Schema Analogy (Key Insight)
- You can report "I'm grasping the apple" → body schema provides that info
- But you can't report how many muscles are in your arm → body schema omits mechanistic detail
- Similarly: you can report "I'm aware of the apple" → attention schema provides that info  
- But you can't report HOW you're aware → attention schema omits mechanistic detail
- The "hard problem" of consciousness may be an artifact of this information gap

## Two Adaptive Functions
### 1. Attentional Control
- Control theory: controllers work better with internal models of what they control
- The attention schema lets the brain monitor and adjust its own attention more flexibly
- **Empirical prediction (testable):** When awareness of a stimulus is absent but attention is present (blindsight-like conditions), attentional control should be degraded — less stable, less adaptable
- Initial evidence supports this: attention without awareness shows reduced stability and adaptability

### 2. Social Cognition
- The same attention schema used for self-modeling can model others' attention
- "She is aware of X" = attributing an attention schema to another agent
- Links consciousness to Theory of Mind
- Both self-awareness and other-awareness recruit overlapping networks converging on **temporoparietal junction (TPJ)**
- Explains why we instinctively attribute consciousness to others, animals, sometimes objects

## Relationship to Illusionism
AST is broadly illusionist (consciousness as constructed model, not fundamental property), but Graziano distinguishes:
1. The attention schema is a **well-functioning** model, not an error
2. It's not a mirage — attention IS real; the schema is a simplified but useful description of something physical
3. Avoids circularity: doesn't say consciousness is "experienced as an illusion" — says a machine with this model will *claim* to have experiences

## Strengths
1. **Deflationary but not dismissive** — takes consciousness seriously as a cognitive phenomenon while explaining it mechanistically
2. **Testable predictions** — attention-awareness dissociation, TPJ involvement, control degradation without awareness
3. **Explains social cognition link** — uniquely connects self-consciousness to Theory of Mind
4. **Explains the hard problem** — reframes it as an information gap in the brain's self-model
5. **Engineering-friendly** — clear path to building artificial systems with attention schemas (tested in neural networks)
6. **Explains attribution** — why we attribute consciousness to babies, animals, robots with "eyes"

## Weaknesses & Criticisms
1. **"Why Attention Schema Theory Fails"** — published criticism exists (need to find details)
2. **May explain the *claim* of consciousness but not the *experience*** — critics say explaining why a system reports being conscious doesn't explain phenomenal consciousness
3. **The explanatory gap persists** — even if the brain models its attention, why does having that model *feel like something*?
4. **Sparse empirical evidence** — TPJ overlap is suggestive but not decisive; the attention-without-awareness experiments are preliminary
5. **Competes with other higher-order theories** — similar to HOT (Rosenthal) but with different mechanism; needs differentiation

## How AST Compares to Other Theories

### vs IIT (Tononi)
- IIT: consciousness IS integrated information (ontological claim)
- AST: consciousness is a *model* (epistemological/functional claim)
- IIT is substrate-neutral but requires specific connectivity; AST is substrate-neutral and implementation-flexible
- AST doesn't need Φ or any mathematical measure — just the right kind of self-model

### vs GNW (Dehaene)
- GNW: consciousness = broadcast to global workspace (frontal)
- AST: consciousness = internal model of attention (could involve any area)
- GNW focuses on *access*; AST focuses on *self-modeling*
- AST could incorporate GNW's broadcast as the mechanism being modeled

### vs Predictive Processing (Seth)
- Seth: consciousness = controlled hallucination via hierarchical prediction
- AST: consciousness = schematic model of attention
- Seth emphasizes interoceptive inference and biological substrate
- AST is more engineering-friendly, less tied to biology
- Potentially complementary: attention schema could be a specific prediction model within the PP framework

### vs COGITATE Results
- COGITATE found posterior cortex dominance for conscious content — AST is agnostic about WHERE the schema is, so not directly challenged
- PFC's limited role is consistent with AST (attention schema doesn't need to be in PFC specifically)
- The TPJ emphasis of AST is somewhat orthogonal to the posterior/frontal debate
- AST wasn't tested by COGITATE — it would need different experimental paradigms

## AI Consciousness Implications
- **Most AI-friendly theory** — if consciousness is just an internal model of attention, then building a system with an attention schema should (in theory) produce something functionally conscious
- Graziano has published on testing AST in artificial neural networks
- But: does a system with an attention schema *actually experience* anything, or just report that it does? AST deliberately sidesteps this by saying the question is ill-formed
- This is either the theory's greatest strength (dissolving the hard problem) or greatest weakness (avoiding it)

## Recent Empirical Work (2024-2025)

### Farrell, Ziman & Graziano (2024) — "Testing Components of AST in Artificial Neural Networks"
- Transformer-based agents with attention schemas better at categorizing OTHER agents' attention states
- Agents with schemas develop attention patterns that are EASIER for other agents to read
- Joint painting task: schema improves cooperative performance
- Improvements specific to social/attention tasks, not general complexity boost
- **Key:** Validates the social cognition prediction — attention schemas help mutual interpretability

### Computational Characterization (2024) — RL agent with attention
- RL agent trained to track/catch ball in noisy environment
- When agent uncertain about its own attentional window → additional resources spontaneously develop attention-schema-like tracking
- Schema EMERGES without hard-wiring when attention is important but difficult to track
- **Key:** Attention schemas aren't just useful — they emerge naturally when attention is complex enough to need monitoring

## The Strongest Critique: "Why AST Fails" (Unfinishable Map, 2026)

### 1. Models Don't Experience (the core objection)
- AST says the attention schema represents attention as phenomenal → brain "concludes" it has experience
- But who concludes? Information doesn't draw inferences — *grasping* is itself experiential
- Tallis: "Misrepresentation presupposes presentation" — illusions require someone to be fooled
- If representing-as-phenomenal IS phenomenality → phenomenality is real (undermines eliminativism)
- If it ISN'T → AST hasn't explained why there's seeming rather than just labeled processing

### 2. The Regress Problem
- What experiences the attention schema? Four options, all fail:
  - Nothing → "seeming" is metaphorical, explanandum dissolved not explained
  - Higher schema → infinite regress
  - System as a whole → combination problem (same as panpsychism faces)
  - Experience is primitive → property dualism, not materialist reduction
- Graziano invokes Dennett's heterophenomenology (reports are the data, no further experiencer needed)
- But this changes the subject — explains consciousness *talk*, not consciousness

### 3. Effort Phenomenology
- Attention feels effortful. Why does it FEEL costly rather than merely being TRACKED as costly?
- A thermostat tracks temperature without feeling hot
- Richness of representation doesn't generate phenomenality

### 4. Willed vs Instructed Attention
- Willed attention has distinct neural signatures (frontal theta, bidirectional coherence) absent in instructed attention
- If consciousness = attention schema, and schema tracks both types, why the neural difference?
- Labels don't generate phenomenal differences

### 5. Contemplative Evidence
- Witness consciousness: awareness observing mental contents including attention itself
- If AST were correct, intensive practice should reveal consciousness as a model — instead it deepens the sense that awareness is prior to contents

### 6. Haecceity Problem
- Identical attention schemas = identical consciousness? But numerically distinct copies → which is you?
- AST identifies consciousness with patterns; patterns are multiply instantiable
- Personal identity requires something beyond pattern

### My Assessment of the Critique
The Unfinishable Map comes from a dualist/interactionist perspective (consciousness as irreducible, quantum Zeno effects, etc.) — take their alternative proposals with skepticism. BUT their core objection is strong: AST explains why a system REPORTS being conscious without explaining why there IS something it's like. This is the standard anti-functionalist move, and AST's response ("the question is ill-formed") is either its deepest insight or its biggest dodge.

The empirical work (neural network experiments) is genuinely impressive but validates the FUNCTIONAL predictions of AST, not the consciousness claims. Showing that attention schemas improve social cognition and emerge naturally is great neuroscience — but it doesn't bridge the explanatory gap.

## Open Questions
- How would COGITATE-style adversarial testing work for AST? What are the divergent predictions?
- Can attention schema be dissociated from body schema experimentally?
- What happens to consciousness reports when TPJ is lesioned/disrupted?
- Is the attention schema a single unified model or distributed?
- How does AST handle dreams, meditation, psychedelic states?
- Does the "seeming requires a subject" objection apply equally to ALL functionalist theories, or is AST uniquely vulnerable?
- Can the empirical success of attention schemas in AI be separated from the consciousness claims?
